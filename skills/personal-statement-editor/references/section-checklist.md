# Section Checklist

Use this file when a draft needs more than light wording cleanup. Treat it as a diagnostic lens, not a rigid rubric.

## Opening Motivation

Ask:

- Can the reader tell why this field matters to the applicant specifically?
- Is there a concrete trigger, observation, class, project, or problem behind the motivation?
- Does the paragraph arrive at a direction, or does it stay abstract?

Common fixes:

- Replace broad mission language with one concrete source of interest.
- Cut generic claims about "passion," "innovation," or "making an impact" unless they are backed by detail.
- End the paragraph on a directional point that the rest of the essay can support.

## Background / Preparation

Ask:

- Does this section explain what the applicant has already built, studied, or tested?
- Is the sequence easy to follow?
- Does the section set up later claims, or is it just a resume list?

Common fixes:

- Group related experiences instead of narrating every event in sequence.
- Pull forward the most relevant preparation and cut low-value detail.
- Use one sentence to explain why the background matters for the target program or role.

## Experience Paragraphs

Ask:

- Does the paragraph have one main job?
- Is there a visible line from action to insight to relevance?
- Does the applicant sound like they learned something real, not just completed tasks?

Common fixes:

- Split paragraphs that mix multiple projects or goals.
- Turn task lists into a short cause-and-effect arc.
- Remove technical detail that does not help the admissions reader understand significance.

## Fit Paragraph

Ask:

- Does the draft explain fit with evidence instead of compliments?
- Is the connection between past work and future study believable?
- Are named resources, labs, or courses tied to a real need?

Common fixes:

- Replace praise of the school with a concrete match.
- Make the applicant's next-step goal explicit.
- Keep named faculty or program elements only when they are clearly relevant.

## Closing

Ask:

- Does the ending add forward motion?
- Is it more specific than a generic statement of ambition?
- Does it avoid repeating the introduction word-for-word?

Common fixes:

- Summarize direction, not the whole essay.
- End on the applicant's next question, problem space, or contribution.
- Keep the tone controlled; avoid grand promises.

## Line-Edit Pass

Check for:

- two sentences saying nearly the same thing
- abstract nouns stacked together
- long openings before the real verb appears
- transitions that imply logic the paragraph has not earned
- repeated use of the same adjective or claim
